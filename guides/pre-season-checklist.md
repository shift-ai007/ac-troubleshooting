# Pre-Season AC Checklist: Getting Your System Ready for South Florida Summer

June is here, and if you haven't checked your AC system since last cooling season, now is the time. Every year we see the same pattern: the first 95-degree day hits, a system that was "fine last year" suddenly can't keep up, and homeowners pay emergency rates for a fix that preventive maintenance would have caught.

Here's the checklist our technicians run through at the start of every season.

## 1. Test Run Before You Need It

Don't wait for the first heatwave. Pick a day when it's 80°F+ outside, set your thermostat 5 degrees below room temperature, and let the system run for a full cycle. Walk the house while it's running. Note any rooms that aren't cooling evenly, strange noises, or smells from the vents.

**Catch it early** — catching a problem during a test run means you schedule a repair at regular rates, not emergency rates.

## 2. Clean or Replace the Filter

This costs $5-20 and fixes more "no cooling" problems than any single thing. South Florida's summer humidity and pollen load is brutal on filters. If you can't remember when you last changed it, change it now.

**Pro tip:** Set a recurring reminder on your phone for every 30 days during cooling season (May through October). The filter is usually behind a grille in the hallway ceiling, a slot in the furnace/air handler, or in a filter rack near the indoor unit.

## 3. Clear Around the Outdoor Unit

Go outside and look at the condenser unit. South Florida storms blow palm fronds, leaves, and debris against the unit's coils. Trim any vegetation back at least 24 inches from all sides. Remove any debris you can see through the top grille. Gently hose the exterior coils from the inside out.

**Why it matters:** Dirty coils can increase your energy consumption by 20-30% because the system has to work harder to reject heat.

## 4. Check the Condensate Drain

Pour a cup of white vinegar down the condensate drain line (the PVC pipe coming from your indoor unit). Wait 15 minutes, then flush with water. This prevents algae buildup that clogs the drain and triggers the safety shutoff.

**When it's too late:** If you see water staining on your ceiling near the air handler, the drain is already clogged and water is backing up. [Professional AC maintenance](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) includes clearing the drain line and checking the safety float switch.

## 5. Inspect Visible Ductwork

If you have attic access, take a look at the flex ducts connected to your air handler. In South Florida attics reach 120°F+, which can soften duct tape and cause connections to separate.

**What to look for:** Any disconnected ducts, crushed sections, or ducts resting on sharp objects. Even a small disconnect can leak 20% of your cooled air into the attic.

## 6. Test the Thermostat

Make sure your thermostat's programming is correct for summer. Check that the schedule switches to "away" temperatures during the day (82°F is fine when no one's home) and cools back down before you return.

**Older thermostats:** A mechanical thermostat that's more than 10 years old may not be accurate anymore. [Upgrading to a programmable thermostat](https://ac-repair.today/services/thermostat-installation/) can save 10-15% on cooling costs.

## 7. Listen for Unusual Noises

Turn the system on and listen at the indoor unit and outdoor unit. Grinding, screeching, or loud humming means something needs attention. Most AC components give audible warning signs before they fail.

**Don't ignore it:** A failing blower motor bearing sounds like a high-pitched squeal at first. Running it until the motor seizes turns a $200 bearing replacement into an $800 motor replacement — or a $4,000 full system if it happens during a heatwave and you need an [emergency AC repair in Miami](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/).

## The One Thing That Saves You the Most Money

Schedule a professional tune-up once a year before summer starts. A good technician will clean the evaporator and condenser coils, check refrigerant charge, test the capacitor and contactor, lubricate moving parts, and verify proper airflow. It costs about $100-200 and routinely finds issues that would have turned into $1,000+ emergency calls in July.
