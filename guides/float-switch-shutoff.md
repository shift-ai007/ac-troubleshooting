# AC Keeps Shutting Off? Diagnosing a Tripped Condensate Float Switch

You walk past the thermostat, notice the house is warm, and find the air handler completely dead — no fan, no cooling, no error on the thermostat. The breaker is fine. An hour later it mysteriously works again, then dies once more by evening. Before you assume the worst about your compressor, check the cheapest and most common culprit in South Florida: the **condensate float switch**, also called a safety float switch, overflow switch, or wet switch.

This little device is the #1 reason AC systems shut themselves off in our region, and it is doing exactly what it was designed to do. This guide walks you through what it is, why it trips, and what you can safely check before calling a pro.

## What the Float Switch Does and Why It Exists

Your air conditioner pulls enormous amounts of moisture out of South Florida's humid air. That water collects in a drain pan and exits through a condensate drain line. When the line clogs — and in our climate, it clogs constantly with algae and biofilm — the water backs up. Left unchecked, that overflow ruins drywall, floors, and ceilings, and can total an air handler in an attic.

The float switch prevents that flood. It sits in the drain line or pan, and when water rises, a small float lifts and **opens the 24V control circuit**, instantly shutting the system down. No cooling is annoying; a collapsed ceiling is a disaster. The switch is the system protecting your home.

## Symptoms That Point to the Float Switch

| Symptom | Float switch likely? |
|---------|----------------------|
| System dead, breaker NOT tripped | Yes — classic signature |
| Works after sitting, fails again later | Yes — water level rising and falling |
| Thermostat screen blank (on some setups) | Possibly — switch can cut thermostat power |
| Outdoor unit runs but no indoor air | Less likely — points elsewhere |
| Standing water near the air handler | Yes — overflow already happening |
| Musty smell with the shutdowns | Yes — clogged, dirty drain line |

The tell-tale pattern is **intermittent shutdowns with a healthy breaker**. Heat causes more runtime, more condensate, faster backup — which is why it so often strikes on the hottest afternoons, exactly when you need cooling most.

## What You Can Safely Check Yourself

These steps involve no electrical or refrigerant work and are appropriate for a careful homeowner:

1. **Look for standing water.** Check the drain pan under the air handler and the secondary pan (attic units). Water present strongly confirms a drainage problem.
2. **Find the float switch.** It's a small plastic device clipped into the PVC drain line near the air handler, often with two thin wires.
3. **Locate the drain line cleanout** — usually a capped PVC tee near the indoor unit.
4. **Clear the clog.** Many homeowners attach a wet/dry vacuum to the outdoor end of the condensate line and pull the clog out. Then pour a cup of distilled white vinegar (not bleach, which can damage some components) into the cleanout to break down algae.
5. **Let the pan drain and dry.** Once the water level drops, the float falls and the switch closes — the system should restart on its own.

If clearing the line restores normal operation, you've solved it. To keep it from recurring, flush the line with vinegar monthly — preventive drain maintenance is the single most effective DIY habit in a humid climate.

## When It's Time to Call a Professional

Clearing a clog is a temporary fix. The shutdowns will come back — usually worse — if the underlying cause isn't addressed. Call a licensed technician when:

- The line clogs again within weeks (it may be improperly sloped or undersized)
- You find water but **no** float switch installed (a serious code and safety gap)
- The switch doesn't reset after the water drains (failed switch)
- There's mold, a heavily fouled drain pan, or a rusted secondary pan
- You're not comfortable working around an attic air handler

## Prevent the Next Shutdown

- **Flush the condensate line monthly** with a cup of distilled white vinegar.
- **Keep a wet/dry vacuum handy** for quick clog clearing.
- **Replace the air filter on schedule** — a dirty filter increases condensate and dust that feeds drain-line slime.
- **Have the drain and float switch checked** during seasonal [AC maintenance](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) so problems are caught before the heat of summer.

A tripped float switch is the system doing its job — protecting your home from water damage. Treat it as a reminder that the drain needs attention, clear the line, and build a monthly flush into your routine. It's one of the few AC problems where a few minutes of homeowner maintenance prevents both an expensive repair and a ruined ceiling.
