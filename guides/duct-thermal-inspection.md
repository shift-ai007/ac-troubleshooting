# Duct Thermal Inspection: Finding Leaks Without Fancy Tools

Leaky ducts are the most common reason a South Florida home's AC runs twice as long as it should. The standard fix — mastic sealant — is cheap and permanent, but finding the leaks in an attic that hits 140°F requires a methodical approach. This guide covers thermal inspection techniques that work without a thermal camera, plus the professional-grade infrared method for when you want to be certain.

## Why Duct Leaks Matter More in Florida

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| Attic temperature (140°F+) | Every cubic foot of conditioned air lost pulls in 140°F attic air through infiltration — the system has to cool it from scratch |
| High humidity | Leaking ducts depressurize the living space, drawing hot, humid outdoor air through every window gap and electrical outlet |
| Slab-foundation homes | Ducts run exclusively in the attic (no basement), so EVERY duct is in unconditioned space |
| Year-round cooling | 8–10 months of continuous strain vs 3–4 months in northern climates |

A 20% duct leak in a Miami home costs roughly $400–600 per year in wasted energy, plus the comfort cost of rooms that never get cool.

## The Three Leak Detection Methods

### Method 1: The Hand Sweep (Free, Attic Required)

You'll need: a hot day, the system running in COOL, and access to the attic.

1. Set the thermostat to COOL and let the system run for at least 10 minutes
2. In the attic, move insulation aside to expose duct joints (don't compress the insulation — it won't spring back)
3. Run your bare hand slowly around every seam, junction, and boot connection
4. **What you're feeling for**: a cold air stream escaping. Even a small leak produces a noticeable cool breeze against a sweaty hand in a 130°F attic

**Where to focus**:
- **Plenum-to-trunk connection** — The main junction where the air handler connects to the supply trunk line. This is the largest pressure drop in the system. Most common leak location.
- **Branch takeoffs** — Where flexible duct connects to the rigid trunk. These are often secured with a zip tie that loosens over time.
- **Boot-to-drywall seams** — Where the metal register boot meets the ceiling. A common source of hidden leakage into the attic cavity.
- **Return duct connections** — Leaks here pull hot attic air directly into the return, bypassing the filter entirely.

**Marking leaks**: Use painter's tape to flag every leak site. You'll seal them afterward.

### Method 2: The Tissue Test (Intermediate)

A refinement of the hand sweep. Cut a 2"×2" piece of tissue paper or a single-ply paper towel. Hold it with tweezers or a straightened paper clip near suspected leak sites.

- A tissue that **moves toward** the leak = supply side leakage (bypressurized air escaping)
- A tissue that **moves away** from the leak = return side leakage (vacuum pulling attic air in)

The tissue test is more sensitive than the hand and works for smaller leaks you'd miss with touch alone.

### Method 3: Thermal Camera Inspection (Professional)

This is the gold standard. A trained HVAC technician uses a thermal camera to scan ducts with the system running:

- **Supply leaks** appear as cold plumes against the warm attic background
- **Return leaks** appear as warm streaks on the duct surface (attic air being pulled in)
- **Insulation gaps** show as hot spots on the duct skin

**Why professional thermal imaging beats DIY**: The camera sees leaks you can't reach (buried under insulation, inside chases, between joists) and documents them with photos and temperature measurements. A complete duct thermal inspection costs $150–$300 and identifies issues that would otherwise go unnoticed for years.

## Sealing Leaks: Mastic, Not Tape

If you find leaks accessible in the attic:

### Do Use:
- **Mastic (paintable duct sealant)** — A thick, water-based paste that remains flexible and forms a permanent airtight bond. Apply with a 2" paintbrush over the joint (±1" overlap). Cost: ~$15 per quart. Any hardware store carries it.
- **Mastic + fiberglass mesh tape** — For larger gaps (>1/4"), embed fiberglass mesh tape into the first mastic layer, then top-coat.
- **Aerosol-based sealing (Aeroscal)** — Professional-only. Seals all leaks from inside the duct using latex particles. $800–1,200 for a typical home but catches leaks you can't see. Worth considering if the home has persistent comfort issues despite sealed visible leaks.

### Do NOT Use:
- **Duct tape** — Despite the name, duct tape fails in 1–3 years in attic heat. The adhesive degrades and the tape falls off. Never use it for duct sealing.
- **Standard aluminum foil tape** — Better than duct tape but still fails at seams under pressure. Only use UL-181-rated foil tape with the listing printed on the backing.
- **Spray foam** — Not rated for ductwork. Can off-gas volatiles into the airstream and some formulations degrade the duct material.

## The Return Side Is More Important Than Supply

Most homeowners think about supply leaks (cold air escaping into the attic). But return-side leaks are actually more damaging:

- A return leak pressurizes the attic relative to the house, making your system pull humid attic air through the leak rather than pulling return air from the living space
- Return leaks bypass the filter — every particle, fiberglass piece, and mold spore in that attic goes straight to the blower wheel and evaporator coil
- A return leak increases latent load (humidity) because you're pulling 80% RH attic air through a coil that was sized for 50% RH return air

**The #1 return-side location to check**: The junction between the return drop (the metal box above the air handler) and the air handler cabinet itself. In many installations, this was never sealed with mastic — only screwed together, leaving a 1/4" gap that pulls hot attic air into the system.

## When to Call a Professional

Schedule a professional duct inspection if:

- Your electric bill is $100–200/month higher than neighbors with a similar-sized home
- The temperature difference between the thermostat location and the farthest bedroom is 5°F+
- One room is consistently 4–6°F warmer in summer despite ducts being fully open
- You've replaced a blower motor more than once (excessive runtime from unsealed ducts wears motors faster)
- You smell attic (dust, fiberglass) from any supply vent

A licensed [AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/) technician can perform a duct airtightness test that measures total leakage in CFM at 25 Pa — the industry standard. If leak rate exceeds 20% of system airflow, duct sealing (or replacement if ducts are fiberboard or old flex) should be prioritized before replacing any equipment.

## Quick Summary

1. Run the hand sweep and tissue test on accessible attic ductwork
2. Seal every leak with mastic (never tape)
3. Return-side leaks are 2× as damaging as supply-side
4. If you find 6+ leaks or can't reach the main trunk lines, hire a pro
5. Combine a duct inspection with your [annual AC maintenance visit](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) for efficiency

Duct sealing is the single highest-ROI home efficiency improvement in South Florida — a $50 mastic kit and an hour in the attic can cut your cooling bill by 15–25% for the life of the system.
