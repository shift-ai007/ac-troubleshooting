# Intermittent Cooling Loss: Diagnostics for "Works Sometimes" AC Problems

An AC that works fine for hours or days then suddenly stops — only to start working again on its own — is one of the most frustrating problems a homeowner faces. The system isn't completely broken, so what's causing it to fail unpredictably?

This guide walks through the systematic approach to diagnosing intermittent cooling loss, organized by most likely cause for South Florida homes.

## The Diagnostic Framework

When an intermittent problem appears, your first task is to identify **what changes when it stops working**. Temperature? Humidity? Noise? The answer dramatically narrows the search.

### Step 1: Identify the Failure Mode

| Symptom | Most Likely Cause | Speed to Investigate |
|---|---|---|
| Outdoor unit runs but no cool air | Refrigerant leak (slow) or metering device intermittent | Medium |
| Indoor fan runs but outdoor unit off | Contactor, capacitor, thermostat, or safeties tripping | **High** |
| Nothing runs (system completely dead) | Thermostat, transformer, breaker, or safety switch | **High** |
| Ice on refrigerant lines or indoor coil | Airflow restriction or low refrigerant | Medium |
| Cools for hours then stops on hot days | Compressor thermal overload (high head pressure) | Low (hire pro) |

### Step 2: Reproduce the Next Episode

You cannot fix an intermittent problem you cannot observe. When the system fails:

1. **Check the thermostat** — Is it powered? Does it show "Cool On" (or equivalent call for cooling)? Is the set-point actually below room temperature? A blank or battery-low thermostat explains everything.
2. **Check the outdoor unit** — Is the condenser fan spinning? Can you hear the compressor running (a low hum)? If the fan spins but no compressor hum, or vice versa, that's your clue.
3. **Check the indoor unit** — Is the air handler/indoor fan running? Is the drain pan dry? A tripped float switch kills the system silently.
4. **Check the breaker panel** — A tripped breaker (it will be in the middle or OFF position) means a hard fault. A breaker that trips repeatedly and resets on its own after cooling down indicates an intermittent short or ground fault.

## Most Common Intermittent Faults in South Florida AC Systems

### 1. Tripped Condensate Float Switch

The float switch is the #1 cause of intermittent "works then stops" in South Florida homes. When the condensate drain clogs, water backs up in the drain pan. The float switch rises and kills the system to prevent overflow.

**Why it's intermittent**: The water slowly evaporates overnight (drain pan clears), the float drops, and next morning the AC works again. Afternoon humidity + heavy cooling load refills the pan → system stops again in the evening.

**Diagnostic check**: Locate the drain pan under the indoor air handler. Is there standing water? A wet/dry vacuum can clear the drain line at the condensate drain pipe outside. Pouring a cup of water down the line confirms it's clear (water should flow freely out the exit).

**Quick fix**: Clear the condensate drain line with a wet/dry vacuum at the outdoor drain termination, then flush with 1 cup of distilled white vinegar. Repeat every 3 months as prevention.

### 2. Thermal Overload Trip (Compressor)

The compressor has an internal thermal overload that physically opens the circuit when the compressor overheats. On 95°F+ South Florida afternoons, a compressor with minor refrigerant undercharge, dirty condenser coils, or a failing start component can hit its thermal limit and shut down.

**Why it's intermittent**: The compressor cools down (typically 30-90 minutes), the overload resets, and the compressor runs again — often until the next hot afternoon.

**Diagnostic clues**:
- Works fine in mornings and evenings, fails in peak afternoon heat
- Outdoor condenser fan runs but compressor is off (no low hum)
- "Cools for 2 hours, then stops" pattern during summer
- When restarting, you hear a humming sound for 5-10 seconds before the compressor starts (hard-starting)

**Run capacitor test**: A failing run capacitor causes the compressor to run hotter and draw higher amperage, which triggers thermal overload sooner. If the system is less reliable on the hottest days, capacitance is often the culprit.

### 3. Loose or Corroded Electrical Connections

South Florida's salt air, high humidity, and thermal cycling (metal expanding/contracting as it heats and cools) causes screw terminals on the contactor, disconnect, and breaker to loosen over time. A loose connection generates heat, which increases resistance, which creates more heat until the connection fails.

**Why it's intermittent**: The connection works when cool but expands open (loses contact) when hot. An hour after the system shuts down, it cools, the connection re-makes, and the system runs again.

**Diagnostic clues**:
- Burned smell near the outdoor unit
- Soot or discoloration around the electrical disconnect
- Warm or hot breaker when nothing else is drawing power
- AC works after being off all night but fails after running for hours

**This is a fire risk**. Loose connections generate enough heat to melt wiring insulation and start electrical fires. If you suspect this, have a licensed electrician or HVAC contractor inspect immediately.

### 4. Surging or Sagging Power from the Grid

South Florida's aging power infrastructure, combined with everyone's AC running simultaneously on 95°F days, creates voltage sags (brownouts) that cause contactors to chatter, compressors to drop out, and safety circuits to trip.

**Why it's intermittent**: Voltage sags are unpredictable — they depend on grid load in your neighborhood at any given moment.

**Diagnostic clues**:
- Multiple appliances (AC, refrigerator, lights) misbehave simultaneously
- Lights dim noticeably when the AC kicks on
- The AC drops out during afternoon peak hours (3-6 PM)
- The contactor chatters (rapid clicking) before stopping

**Solutions**: A whole-house surge protector, a hard-start kit, and in severe cases a low-voltage disconnect that protects the compressor during brownouts.

### 5. Failing Run Capacitor

Already detailed in our [capacitor testing guide](capacitor-testing-guide.md), but worth reiterating: a capacitor that has degraded to the margins of its tolerance range will work when cool but fail as internal temperature rises.

**Why it's intermittent**: The capacitor's microfarad (µF) rating drifts as it heats up. At twilight or in the morning, the capacitor provides just enough capacitance. By 3 PM, heat pushes it below the threshold needed for the compressor to start.

## When Intermittent Problems Require a Professional

Some intermittent faults cannot be diagnosed without specialized equipment and experience:

- **Intermittent refrigerant leak** — A slow leak that only causes symptoms on the hottest days requires a refrigerant charge measurement, superheat/subcooling calculation, and leak search. This is not a DIY job.
- **Compressor winding fault** — Intermittent short-to-ground in the compressor windings only appears under load. It requires a megohmmeter (megger) that most homeowners don't own.
- **Failing circuit board or communicating thermostat** — Variable-speed and communicating systems have control boards that can fail in subtle, intermittent ways. Error code logging requires the manufacturer's service tool.
- **Duct leakage that only matters at peak load** — A minor duct leak that's normally acceptable becomes a problem when outdoor temperatures hit 95°F+ and the system can't keep up.
