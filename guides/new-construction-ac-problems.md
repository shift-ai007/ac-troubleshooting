# New Construction AC Problems: Why a Brand-New System Fails in a Brand-New Home

South Florida is building homes faster than any region in the country, and new construction AC systems fail at a surprisingly high rate — often within the first 12-24 months. The problem isn't usually the equipment itself. It's how the system was selected, installed, and what happens to a new home during its first year.

If you moved into a new Florida home in the last two years and your AC is struggling, you're not alone. Here's what goes wrong, how to diagnose it, and what to do about it.

## Why New Construction AC Systems Fail in Florida

### The Low-Bid Problem

New home builders, especially production builders operating across large developments in Miami-Dade and Broward, bid HVAC work to the lowest-qualified subcontractor. The winning bid often cuts corners in ways that aren't visible during a final walkthrough but create chronic problems within months:

- **Cheapest equipment tier**: Builders install the lowest-efficiency units that meet code minimum (14 SEER2, or whatever the current minimum is). These units have cheaper compressors, thinner coil metals, and less cabinet insulation.
- **Minimum labor hours**: The install crew is paid per house, not per quality metric. This means rushed brazing, undertorqued connections, and missed details.
- **No commissioning**: The system is started, verified to blow cold air, and signed off. Refrigerant charge, airflow measurement, and static pressure are almost never checked.

### The "One-Size-Fits-Some" Problem

Production home communities offer 3-4 floor plans. The HVAC design is often reused across identical plans without accounting for orientation:

- A home with west-facing windows and dark roof shingles needs 15-20% more cooling capacity than the same plan with north-south orientation and light tile
- Corner lots have more exterior wall exposure than interior units in villas or townhomes
- Homes with bonus rooms, high ceilings, or open stairwells have different load profiles than standard layouts

Most production builders do not perform individual Manual J load calculations per lot. They copy the design from the first unit of that model and reuse it.

### Construction Debris in the Ductwork

This is the single most common new-construction AC problem in South Florida:

During construction, the ductwork is often installed before drywall, flooring, and trim work are complete. Drywall dust, sawdust, insulation fibers, and even tools and lunch wrappers end up inside the ductwork. The system is then started up — and all that debris blows directly into:

- The evaporator coil (coating it with an insulating layer of dust)
- The blower wheel (loading it with debris that unbalances the motor)
- The supply registers (restricting airflow from day one)

**Check this first**: Remove a supply register cover near the air handler and shine a flashlight inside. If you see construction debris, loose drywall dust, or foreign objects, the entire duct system needs professional cleaning.

### Improper Refrigerant Charge

New construction HVAC crews are under pressure to finish quickly. Many skip the critical step of weighing in the refrigerant charge according to the manufacturer's specification. Instead, they use the "feel the lines and check the pressures" method — which is notoriously inaccurate, especially in Florida's high-ambient conditions.

Symptoms of improper charge in a new system:
- System runs 45+ minutes without reaching the thermostat setpoint on an 88°F day
- Supply vents blow "coolish" (65-68°F) instead of cold (50-55°F)
- Compressor cycles on and off more than 4 times per hour
- Ice forms on the larger refrigerant line at the outdoor unit

An undercharged system loses efficiency and the compressor runs hotter. An overcharged system stresses the compressor and can cause premature failure. Both are common in new Florida construction.

### Oversized Equipment (The Most Common Problem)

Counterintuitively, bigger is not better for AC systems in South Florida. An oversized system:
1. Cools the house quickly but doesn't run long enough to remove humidity
2. Leaves the home feeling "clammy" at 76°F while a properly sized system feels comfortable at 78°F
3. Short-cycles (runs 8-12 minutes, shuts off, restarts 10 minutes later)
4. Wears out the compressor faster from frequent start-stop cycles

Production builders oversize for a simple reason: an oversized system is less likely to get callback complaints about "not cooling enough." The trade-off is poor humidity control and higher energy bills. A [professional AC contractor](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/) can verify whether your system was properly sized through a Manual J calculation.

## Common New Construction AC Problems — Quick Reference

| Symptom | Most Likely Cause in New Construction | Diagnostic Check |
|---------|--------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Warm air from vents | Construction debris blocking coil | Remove register cover, inspect duct interior |
| Weak airflow | Debris in blower wheel or kinked flex duct | Check accessible flex duct for sharp bends |
| Short cycling (runs 8-12 min, stops, restarts) | Oversized equipment | Measure runtime vs thermostat demand |
| Runs constantly, never satisfies setpoint | Undercharged, undersized, or leaking ducts | Measure delta T (should be 16-22°F) |
| Musty smell from vents | Debris/moisture in ducts from construction | Visual inspection of ducts near air handler |
| Water leaking from air handler | Drain line clogged with construction debris | Check drain pan for standing water |
| High humidity (house feels sticky at 76°F) | Oversized unit or incorrect blower speed | Measure indoor humidity with stand-alone gauge |

## Diagnostic Steps for New Homeowners

### Step 1: Check the Filter Monthly (First Year)

New construction generates ongoing dust. The first year of occupancy produces more filter loading than any subsequent year. Check your filter every 30 days and replace when visibly dirty. Expect to replace it 2-3× more frequently than normal for the first 6-12 months.

### Step 2: Measure Temperature Drop (Delta T)

With the system running for at least 15 minutes:

1. Measure temperature at a supply register nearest the air handler
2. Measure temperature at the return grille
3. Subtract supply from return

**Expected**: 16-22°F
**Too low (<14°F)**: Low refrigerant, dirty coil, or airflow restriction
**Too high (>24°F)**: Very low airflow — restricted filter, dirty blower, or undersized ducts

### Step 3: Locate Your Commissioning Report

Ask your builder for the HVAC commissioning report. This should include:
- Model and serial numbers of all equipment
- Refrigerant charge (subcooling or superheat readings)
- Static pressure measurement
- Airflow measurement (CFM)
- Delta T at time of installation

If the builder cannot provide this, the system was likely never properly commissioned. Consider scheduling an independent [AC maintenance inspection](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) to establish a baseline.

### Step 4: Check Temperature Across Rooms

Walk through the house with a thermometer. More than 4°F difference between the coolest and warmest room suggests:
- Ductwork design flaw (common in open-plan Florida homes)
- Dampered zones not properly balanced
- Flex duct runs that are too long or have too many bends

### Step 5: Verify Your Warranty

Florida new construction AC systems typically carry:
- **Manufacturer warranty**: 5-10 years on compressor, 1-5 years on parts (varies by brand)
- **Builder warranty**: 1-2 years on installation workmanship
- **Statutory warranty**: Florida law implies a 1-year defect warranty for new construction

Document every issue in writing to the builder during the first year. Verbal complaints are not sufficient to establish a warranty claim.

## What to Do If Your New Construction AC Is Failing

### During the Builder's 1-Year Warranty

1. **Document everything in writing** — Email the builder's warranty department with photos, temperature measurements, and descriptions of the problem
2. **Request a third-party inspection** — The builder's HVAC subcontractor will find no fault with their own work. Pay a licensed [AC repair technician](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/) for an independent evaluation and use their report as leverage
3. **Know your rights** — The Florida New Home Warranty Act (FS 553.9) requires builders to remedy defects reported within the warranty period
4. **Escalate for air quality issues** — Construction debris in ducts is a health issue, not just a comfort issue. Florida law takes a stricter view of health-related defects versus performance issues

### After the Builder Warranty Expires

If the problem was never documented during the first year, you may still have recourse under the manufacturer's equipment warranty for parts (compressor, coil, etc.). Labor for repairs will be your responsibility.

Common issues that appear after the first year in new-construction Florida homes:
- **Leaking evaporator coil**: Florida's humidity and aggressive duct cleaning chemicals can cause premature coil failures at 2-4 years
- **Failing capacitor**: New units sometimes ship with marginal capacitors; failure within 18-36 months is common
- **Refrigerant leak at a brazed joint**: Poor installation brazing (rushed or improperly fluxed) develops pinhole leaks at 1-3 years

## Preventing Problems in a New Construction Home

If you're still in the building phase:

- **Request a Manual J load calculation** for your specific lot and elevation
- **Ask for duct cleaning** before closing — include it in the purchase contract
- **Request the commissioning report** before final walkthrough
- **Do a final walkthrough test**: Set the thermostat to 78°F, run for 1 hour, measure delta T, and check all room temperatures
- **Get a pre-closing AC inspection** from an independent [HVAC professional](https://ac-repair.today/) — not the builder's subcontractor

## Summary

New construction AC problems in South Florida are predictable and preventable. The root cause is almost never the equipment — it's the intersection of rushed installation, reused designs, and construction debris. Most issues are fixable with a professional duct cleaning, a refrigerant charge adjustment, or a blower speed change. The key is catching them during the builder's warranty window when the fix is free.

If you're in a new home and your AC isn't performing, you're not imagining it. Document everything, get an independent evaluation, and hold the builder accountable within the warranty period.
