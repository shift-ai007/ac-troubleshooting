# Outdoor Unit Running but the Top Fan Won't Spin: Condenser Fan Motor Diagnostics

You walk outside and your AC's outdoor unit is humming — you can hear the compressor working — but the large fan on top isn't turning. Inside, the air coming from the vents is warm. This is one of the most common AC failures, and it's also one of the most urgent: a compressor running without its condenser fan will overheat and can destroy itself in minutes.

This guide helps you identify whether the problem is the fan motor, the capacitor, or something else, and tells you clearly when to stop and shut the system down.

## Stop First: Why This Is Time-Sensitive

The condenser fan pulls air across the outdoor coil to dump the heat your AC removed from the house. If that fan stops while the compressor keeps running, heat builds up fast. The compressor can trip on its internal overload, and repeated overheating cycles permanently damage it.

**If the fan isn't spinning but the compressor is running and getting hot, turn the system off at the thermostat now.** Then work through the checks below. If you're unsure, treat it as urgent — a seized fan is exactly the kind of failure that justifies [emergency AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/) on a hot day.

## The Quick Stick Test (Safe, No Tools Inside the Unit)

With the system off, look down through the top grille at the fan blade. Now turn the system back on for just a moment and watch:

- **Fan tries to start, twitches, then stops** → most likely a failed **run capacitor** (the most common cause by far).
- **Fan doesn't move at all and you hear a hum** → could be a seized motor or a dead capacitor.
- **Fan blade is hard to spin by hand (system off)** when you nudge it with a stick → the motor bearings are seized; the motor needs replacement.
- **Fan spins freely by hand and starts when you give it a gentle push (system on)** → classic weak-capacitor symptom. Shut it off — a motor that needs a manual push will not last.

Never put your hand into the unit, and never push the blade with the power on except with a long non-conductive stick — and even then, only briefly.

## The Usual Suspects, in Order of Likelihood

### 1. Run Capacitor (most common)
The capacitor gives the fan motor the jolt it needs to start spinning and keeps it running smoothly. Capacitors fail constantly in Florida heat — high temperatures shorten their life. A bulging or leaking top on the capacitor is a dead giveaway. Capacitor replacement is inexpensive and is the first thing a tech will check.

> **Safety note:** Capacitors store a dangerous electrical charge even with the power off. Testing or replacing one is a job for someone trained to discharge it safely. This is not a recommended DIY repair.

### 2. Failed Fan Motor
If the capacitor is good but the motor won't turn — or the blade is stiff/seized by hand — the motor itself has failed. Bearings dry out and corrode, especially in salt-air coastal areas. The motor must be matched on voltage, horsepower, RPM, and rotation direction.

### 3. Contactor Not Pulling In
The contactor is the relay that sends power to the outdoor unit. If it's pitted or stuck, the fan and compressor may not get consistent voltage. Sometimes only partial contact is made, producing odd behavior.

### 4. Debris or Ice Jamming the Blade
Occasionally a stick, leaves, or a chunk of ice blocks the blade. With power off, clear any obvious obstruction before assuming a part has failed.

## What You Can Safely Do Yourself

- Turn the system off to protect the compressor
- Visually check for obstructions in the fan blade (power off)
- Confirm the outdoor disconnect and breaker haven't tripped
- Spray-clean a dirty coil from the outside with a gentle hose (power off) — a clogged coil makes overheating worse

## What Requires a Professional

Capacitor testing, motor replacement, and contactor work all involve stored charge and line voltage.
