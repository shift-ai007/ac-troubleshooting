# Blower Motor Diagnostics: When the Indoor Fan Won't Spin

The blower motor is the part of your air handler that pushes conditioned air through your ducts and out the vents. When it fails, the symptoms are dramatic: no air at the registers, weak or intermittent airflow, a loud hum with no fan movement, or the system tripping off entirely. In South Florida, where the indoor air handler often sits in a hot garage or attic and runs nearly year-round, blower motors take a beating.

This guide helps you figure out whether you're looking at a simple, safe homeowner fix or a failed motor that needs a licensed tech — and how to tell the two common motor types apart, because the diagnosis differs.

> **Safety first:** Anything that requires opening the air handler's electrical compartment, testing energized components, or handling the capacitor is a job for a professional. Always shut off power at the breaker *and* the unit disconnect before opening any panel. A blower capacitor can hold a dangerous charge even with the power off.

## Step 1: Confirm It's Actually the Blower

Before suspecting the motor, rule out the easy stuff:

- **Thermostat set to "Fan: Auto" vs "On".** If you switch the fan to "On" and *still* get no airflow, the problem is real. If the fan runs in "On" but not during cooling, the issue may be the cooling call, not the blower.
- **Air filter completely clogged.** A filter caked solid can choke airflow so badly it feels like the fan isn't running and can overheat the motor. Replace it and retest.
- **Tripped breaker or blown low-voltage fuse.** If the whole system is dead, see the [breaker and electrical panel guide](guides/breaker-and-electrical.md).
- **Float switch tripped.** A full condensate drain pan trips a safety switch that kills the system — extremely common in our humidity. Check the drain line first.

If air still won't move with a clean filter, power confirmed, and the fan set to "On," the blower assembly itself is the suspect.

## Step 2: Identify Your Blower Motor Type

There are two motor families, and they fail differently:

### PSC (Permanent Split Capacitor) Motors
The older, simpler design. A PSC motor is a single- or multi-speed AC motor that relies on a **run capacitor** to start and spin. The tell-tale failure: you hear a **hum** from the air handler but the fan doesn't turn, or it needs a push to get going. That hum-without-spin is the classic signature of a **dead run capacitor** — the cheapest and most common blower-related failure.

### ECM (Electronically Commutated Motor) Motors
The modern, variable-speed, high-efficiency design found in most systems installed in the last decade. An ECM has a small electronic **module** bolted to the motor that controls speed. ECMs are quieter and far more efficient, but they're sensitive to voltage surges (a real concern during South Florida's thunderstorm season) and the control module can fail independently of the motor itself. ECM failures often show as: the blower ramping up and down oddly, refusing to start, or starting then stopping after a few seconds.

You can usually identify the type by the label on the motor or by the presence of a separate plastic control module on the end of an ECM.

## Step 3: Read the Symptoms

| What you observe | Likely cause | Who fixes it |
|------------------|--------------|--------------|
| Loud hum, fan won't spin (PSC) | Failed run capacitor | Pro (capacitor holds charge) |
| Fan spins only after a manual nudge | Failing capacitor or worn bearings | Pro |
| Grinding/squealing while running | Worn motor bearings | Pro |
| Burning smell from air handler | Overheating windings — shut it OFF | Pro, urgently |
| ECM ramps up/down or won't start | ECM control module or motor | Pro |
| No air + clicking from control board | Blower relay or board fault | Pro |
| Weak airflow but motor runs fine | Not the motor — see [weak airflow](guides/weak-airflow.md) | Often DIY |

## Step 4: What Causes Blower Motors to Fail Here

- **Heat stress.** Attic and garage air handlers in South Florida live at 100–140°F. Heat degrades windings, bearings, and electronics faster than in cooler climates.
- **Voltage surges.** Lightning and grid fluctuations are murder on ECM control modules. A whole-home surge protector pays for itself by protecting these expensive boards.
- **Restricted airflow.** A chronically dirty filter or blocked return makes the motor work harder and run hotter, shortening its life. This is why filter discipline matters.
- **Capacitor heat fatigue.** Run capacitors are rated for a temperature ceiling they routinely exceed in our climate, which is why South Florida capacitors often last only 3–5 years.
- **Bearing wear and dust.** Years of dust and condensate-laden air wear out sleeve bearings, producing the grind-and-squeal that precedes total failure.

## Step 5: The Safe Homeowner Checklist

What you **can** safely do:

- Replace the air filter and clear any return-air obstructions
- Reset the breaker once (if it trips again, stop — there's a fault)
- Check and clear the condensate drain / reset the float switch
- Set the thermostat fan to "On" to confirm the motor is the issue
- Listen and smell: a hum-no-spin or a burning odor tells the technician exactly where to look

What you should **never** do: open the blower compartment, discharge or replace the capacitor, or test motor windings while energized. Capacitor and motor work is genuinely dangerous and is reserved for licensed technicians.
