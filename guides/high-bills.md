# High Electric Bills — Energy Costs Suddenly Spiked

If your electric bill jumped and your usage hasn't changed, your AC system is likely working harder than it should.

## Step 1: Rule Out Rate Changes

Compare your bill's kWh usage (not the dollar amount) to the same month last year. If kWh is similar but dollars are higher, the utility raised rates — not an AC problem.

## Step 2: Find the AC-Related Spike

Look at your bill for the peak month comparison. In South Florida, summer AC costs typically run **40-60% of total electric bill**. If your total bill went from $200 to $350, the AC portion likely went from $100 to $220+.

## Common Causes

### Dirty Condenser Coils (Most Common)
The outdoor condenser coils reject heat. When they're coated with dirt, grass, pollen, and road grime, heat can't escape efficiently and the system runs longer. In South Florida, coils need cleaning every 2-3 years minimum.

**DIY check:** Look at the outdoor unit fins through the grille. If the aluminum fins look matted, discolored, or clogged with debris, the coils are dirty.

**Fix:** Professional coil cleaning costs $150-300 and typically improves efficiency by 15-25%.

### Dirty Air Filter
A clogged filter restricts airflow, making the system work harder to push air through. Replace monthly during peak season, every 3 months otherwise.

### Refrigerant Leak
Low refrigerant makes the AC run longer to achieve the same cooling. If your AC runs 16+ hours/day and your bills have crept up over 2-3 months, have refrigerant levels checked.

### Duct Leaks
A 20% duct leakage rate means 20% of your cooled air is going into the attic. That's a 20% efficiency loss. Ducts in unconditioned attics (most Florida homes) leak more as they age.

### System Age
An AC system loses efficiency over time:
- **Year 0-5** — Full rated efficiency
- **Year 5-10** — 5-15% efficiency loss
- **Year 10-15** — 15-30% efficiency loss
- **Year 15+** — Replace. A new 16 SEER system will save 30-50% vs an old 10 SEER unit.

## When to Replace vs Repair

If your system is 12+ years old and needs a major repair ($500+), replacement is usually the better financial decision. A new high-efficiency system pays for itself in 3-5 years of energy savings.
