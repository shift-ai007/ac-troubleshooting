# Ductless Mini-Split Troubleshooting Guide

Ductless mini-split systems are increasingly popular in South Florida for good reason: they provide efficient zoned cooling without ductwork, they're ideal for room additions, garage conversions, and older homes without ducts, and modern inverter-driven models maintain comfort better than traditional systems. But mini-splits have their own unique failure modes that differ from central AC systems.

This guide covers the most common mini-split problems in South Florida's climate and how to diagnose them.

## How Mini-Splits Differ from Central AC

Before diving into troubleshooting, it helps to understand the key differences:

| Component | Central AC | Mini-Split |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Compressor | Outdoor unit only | Outdoor unit (variable-speed inverter) |
| Expansion device | Fixed at outdoor unit (piston or TXV) | Located in each indoor head (EEV) |
| Control wiring | 24V thermostat | Proprietary data communication (2-4 wires) |
| Condensate removal | Gravity drain | Condensate pump (often required) |
| Refrigerant charge | Fixed charge for matched system | Variable by line set length and indoor units |
| Air filter | Central return grille | Washable filters in each indoor unit |

## Common Mini-Split Problems

### 1. Indoor Unit Leaking Water

**The most common mini-split complaint in South Florida** — and the cause is almost never what homeowners expect.

**Likely causes:**
- **Clogged condensate drain** — the condensate pump's drain line gets clogged with algae or biofilm (very common in our humidity). Most mini-splits use a small condensate pump inside or near the indoor unit, and the 3/8" vinyl drain line clogs easily.
- **Dirty evaporator coil** — when the coil fins get packed with dust, condensate forms unevenly and can blow off the coil instead of draining into the pan.
- **Improper installation slope** — the indoor unit must be level or slightly tilted toward the drain connection. If it's tilted away, water pools inside and overflows.
- **Clogged condensate pump** — the pump itself has a small intake screen that gets blocked.
- **Frozen evaporator coil then thaw** — if the coil freezes (from dirty filter or low refrigerant) and then thaws, it can produce more water than the drain can handle at once.

**What you can check:**
1. Remove and clean the washable filter (every 2-4 weeks in summer)
2. Listen for the condensate pump running — it should make a brief humming or clicking sound every few minutes when the unit is cooling. If it's silent, the pump may be dead or the float switch is stuck.
3. Check the drain line exit point — if you see slow dripping or none at all, the line is likely clogged. Flush with a vinegar solution if accessible.

**When to call a pro:** If the drain line is buried in a wall or ceiling, or if the condensate pump needs replacement, call a [licensed AC repair technician](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/). Mini-split condensate pumps are model-specific and improperly wired pumps can flood your home.

### 2. Indoor Unit Blowing Warm Air

**Possible causes:**
- **System in defrost cycle** — mini-splits in heat pump mode periodically reverse to defrost the outdoor coil. This is normal and lasts 5-15 minutes.
- **Incorrect mode** — verify the unit is set to COOL, not FAN or DRY mode
- **Refrigerant leak** — mini-splits have flared connections at both the indoor and outdoor units. These are the #1 refrigerant leak source in mini-splits, and South Florida's temperature cycling (90°F days, 80°F nights) causes flare joints to loosen over time.
- **Compressor communication fault** — the outdoor unit may have stopped responding to the indoor unit's cooling demand
- **Thermistor failure** — the indoor unit's temperature sensor has drifted, telling the system the room is cooler than it actually is

**DIY checks:**
1. Turn the system off for 5 minutes, then back on
2. Change the mode from COOL to FAN and back
3. Clean the filter
4. Check for obvious oil stains on refrigerant line flare nuts (indicates a leak)

### 3. Unit Turns Off by Itself (Short Cycling)

**Likely causes for mini-splits:**
- **Oversized unit for the space** — a 12,000 BTU head in a 150 sq ft room with good insulation will satisfy the thermostat quickly but not run long enough to dehumidify. The unit shuts off, humidity rises, the thermostat calls for cooling again in 5-10 minutes, repeat all day. This is especially common in South Florida garage conversions and ADUs.
- **Thermistor placement** — if the indoor unit's return air thermistor is near a cold spot or directly in the path of supply air, it reads the wrong temperature
- **Dirty outdoor coil** — the outdoor unit's condenser coil gets clogged with cottonwood, grass clippings, or construction dust. The high-pressure switch or thermal protection shuts the system down mid-cycle.
- **Communication wire interference** — mini-splits use low-voltage data cables between indoor and outdoor units. In South Florida, lightning-induced surges can damage the communication circuit without tripping any breaker.

**What to check:**
1. Clean the outdoor unit coil with a garden hose (no pressure washer)
2. Ensure the outdoor unit has 24 inches of clearance on all sides
3. Check if the unit runs longer overnight (when it's cooler) — if so, the problem is sizing or a dirty outdoor coil

### 4. Error Codes and Blinking Lights

Every mini-split brand uses a unique error code system. Here are the most common codes across South Florida's most popular brands:

| Brand | Common Error | Meaning | Typical Fix |
|-------|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **Daikin** | U4 | Communication fault between indoor and outdoor | Check wiring connections |
| **Mitsubishi** | 2500-series | Outdoor unit PCB failure | Professional diagnosis |
| **Fujitsu** | Er1 | Indoor room temperature thermistor fault | Replace thermistor |
| **LG** | CH 02 | Outdoor unit intake pipe thermistor | Check refrigerant charge |
| **Gree** | E6 | Indoor fan motor lock or failure | Motor replacement |
| **Mr. Cool** | E1 | Indoor unit EEPROM error | Power cycle; if persists, replace control board |

**South Florida pattern**: U4 and communication errors spike during afternoon thunderstorms. A whole-home surge protector at the panel is the most effective prevention. Power-cycling (turn off at the disconnect for 5 minutes, then restart) clears most lightning-induced communication errors.

### 5. Mini-Split Not Cooling Enough

**Common causes in South Florida:**
- **Undersized system** — a 9,000 BTU head cannot cool a 400 sq ft South Florida room with west-facing windows, regardless of what the sales brochure says. Our heat load requires Manual J sizing, not rules of thumb.
- **Low refrigerant from flare joint leaks** — as mentioned above, thermal cycling loosens flare connections. This is the #1 refrigerant loss source for mini-splits. A system that cooled fine last year but struggles this year almost certainly has a slow leak at a flare nut.
- **Dirty condenser coil** — mini-split outdoor units sit close to the ground and collect debris faster than central AC condensers
- **Obstructed airflow** — furniture placed too close to the indoor unit blocks the return air path. Mini-splits need 6+ inches of clearance above and in front.

**Measurements to take:**
With the system running in COOL mode at maximum fan speed for at least 15 minutes:
- **Supply temperature**: measure air temperature at the indoor unit's discharge vent
- **Return temperature**: measure air temperature at the unit's top intake grille
- **Delta T**: subtract supply from return — should be 15–20°F. Below 14°F indicates a problem.
- **Outdoor unit line temperature**: the larger insulated pipe (suction line) should feel cold and sweat heavily. If it's barely cool, refrigerant is low. [Professional AC service](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/) is required to locate and repair the leak.

### 6. Condensate Pump Failure

Many mini-split installations require condensate pumps because the indoor unit is mounted on an interior wall or in a finished space without gravity drain access.

**Signs of pump failure:**
- Indoor unit makes a gurgling sound
- Water dripping from the indoor unit even when the filter is clean
- The pump runs continuously (it should cycle on and off)
- Error code for high condensate level (the safety float switch has tripped)

**DIY check:**
1. Turn the system off
2. Open the condensate pump access panel (usually on the bottom-right or behind a service cover)
3. Check if the float moves freely — it can get stuck from biofilm buildup
4. Pour a cup of water into the pump reservoir — the pump should activate and drain it
5. If the pump doesn't run, it likely needs replacement. If it runs but doesn't move water, the drain line is clogged.

**Prevention**: Add 1 cup of white vinegar to the condensate pump reservoir every 3 months. This prevents biofilm growth that clogs both the pump intake and the drain line — especially important in South Florida's warm, humid conditions.

### 7. Wi-Fi and Smart Features Not Working

Modern mini-split controllers rely on:
- The indoor unit's control board
- A Wi-Fi adapter or built-in Wi-Fi module
- The manufacturer's mobile app and cloud service

**Common failures:**
- **Wi-Fi adapter unresponsive** — South Florida lightning storms can damage the adapter's power supply. Power-cycle by unplugging the adapter for 30 seconds.
- **App shows "Offline"** — check if the HVAC itself works from the remote. If the unit works but the app doesn't, the problem is the adapter or your home network, not the system.
- **Schedule lost** — some mini-split brands lose programmed schedules during power outages. This is normal; reprogram after extended outages.

## Mini-Split vs Central AC: When to Call a Pro

| Symptom | DIY? | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Dirty filter | Yes | Wash with mild soap, dry completely |
| Remote not working | Yes | Check batteries, IR path obstructions |
| Water leaking (simple) | Check | Clean filter, check pump, clear visible drain |
| Water leaking (in-wall) | No | Drain line is in wall or ceiling cavity |
| Error code persists through power cycle | No | Indicates failed sensor, board, or communication |
| Low cooling performance | Check | Take delta T measurements, clean outdoor coil |
| Refrigerant leak suspicion | **No** | Requires recovery, repair, evacuation, recharge |
| Compressor not starting | **No** | High-voltage electrical work |
| Inverter board failure | **No** | Model-specific part, high-voltage DC bus |

## Preventive Maintenance for South Florida

Mini-splits need more frequent maintenance than central systems in our climate:

- **Filters**: Clean every 2-4 weeks during peak cooling season (May–October). A dirty filter is the root cause of most mini-split problems.
- **Outdoor coil**: Rinse monthly with a garden hose. Use a coil-safe cleaner twice per year (before summer and before winter if used for heat).
- **Condensate pump**: Add vinegar quarterly. Replace the pump every 3-5 years proactively — a failed pump during peak summer can flood a finished room.
- **Refrigerant flare nuts**: Have a technician check torque annually during your [regular AC maintenance visit](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/). Properly torqued flare nuts rarely leak.
- **Condenser coil anti-corrosion**: If you live within 3 miles of the coast, consider having the outdoor unit coil coated with a corrosion protectant. South Florida's salt air eats mini-split copper coils and aluminum fins faster than central AC condensers because mini-split units are smaller and the coils are more tightly packed.
- **Communication wiring**: Check for signs of rodent damage annually (squirrels and rats chew through mini-split data cables in attics).

## When to Replace vs Repair

Mini-splits typically last 12–15 years, but South Florida's coastal environment can shorten that to 8–12 years. Consider replacement when:

- **Cost of repair exceeds 50% of replacement cost** — a new 12,000 BTU mini-split head costs $800–1,500 installed. A compressor replacement costs $1,500–2,500.
- **The outdoor unit has rusted through** — by year 10, many coastal outdoor units have significant cabinet corrosion.
- **Multiple indoor units have failed** — if 2+ heads on a multi-zone system have compressor or fan issues, a full system replacement is more cost-effective than piecemeal repairs.
- **R-22 system** — R-22 mini-splits from 2010 or earlier are uneconomical to repair due to refrigerant cost.
