# Compressor Thermal Overload Protection: Why Your AC Shuts Off Intermittently in Extreme Heat

A South Florida homeowner's most frustrating AC problem: the system runs fine for hours, then suddenly stops mid-afternoon on the hottest day. Wait 30 minutes, and it kicks back on as if nothing happened. By evening, it runs normally. The next afternoon, the same cycle repeats.

This pattern strongly suggests the **compressor internal thermal overload protector** is tripping. It's a built-in safety device — not a failure in the traditional sense, but a symptom that something else is wrong. This guide explains what the thermal overload does, why it trips, and how to diagnose the root cause.

## What Is the Thermal Overload Protector?

Inside every scroll or reciprocating compressor is a bi-metallic disc — the internal overload protector. It's wired in series with the compressor motor windings and sits in contact with the compressor body. When the internal temperature or current draw exceeds safe limits, the disc heats up, warps, and breaks the electrical circuit. The compressor stops until it cools down enough for the disc to reset.

This mechanism prevents:
- **Motor winding burnout** — heat that breaks down the enamel insulation on copper windings, causing a short
- **Internal mechanical damage** — oil breakdown at high temperatures reduces lubrication
- **Electrical fire** — sustained overcurrent in a locked rotor condition

The overload is a **safety device that correct operation should never trigger**. If it cycles repeatedly, something is pushing the compressor beyond its designed operating range.

## Common Causes of Thermal Overload Tripping

### 1. Condenser Coil Blockage (Most Common in South Florida)

The outdoor coil must reject the heat absorbed from inside your home. When airflow across the coil is restricted:

- **Dirty coil** — layers of dust, pollen, and salt spray insulate the coil surface
- **Vegetation overgrowth** — bushes or grass within 12-24 inches of the condenser
- **Debris blockage** — leaves, pine needles, or cottonwood seeds packed between the coil fins
- **Recycled hot air** — units installed in corners or alcoves where the discharged hot air recirculates through the coil

**Check**: Look at the outdoor unit. Can you see daylight through the coil from multiple angles? Is there a clear path for the fan to pull air in? If the coil looks gray, dusty, or has debris wedged in the fins, it needs cleaning.

### 2. Condenser Fan Motor Failure

The condenser fan is responsible for pulling air across the coil. If it's slowed down or stopped:

- **Weak run capacitor** — the capacitor provides starting torque; a degraded capacitor reduces fan speed
- **Bearing wear** — grinding or squealing noises before complete failure
- **Blade damage** — bent blades move less air and can hit the shroud
- **Motor thermal overload** — the fan motor has its own overload; if it's tripping, the condenser loses all airflow

**Check**: Watch the condenser fan while the unit is running. It should spin freely and at consistent speed. Point a flashlight through the top grille — is the blade hub centered and undamaged?

### 3. Refrigerant Charge Problems

Both overcharge and undercharge can cause high discharge temperatures:

- **Undercharge (low refrigerant)** — reduced mass flow means the compressor runs hotter because less suction gas returns to cool the motor windings. Scroll compressors rely on returning suction gas for motor cooling — drop below the minimum charge and internal temperatures rise rapidly.
- **Overcharge (excess refrigerant)** — liquid refrigerant can flood back to the compressor, diluting oil and causing high head pressure. The high compression ratio generates excess heat.

**Check**: This requires gauges and a professional. If the thermal overload is tripping, it's not a DIY diagnosis — call a licensed technician.

### 4. High Ambient Temperature (The "Peak Heat" Factor)

On days when the outdoor temperature exceeds 95°F, the condenser has to work harder because the temperature difference between the coil and the air is smaller. If the system already has marginal airflow or a mild refrigerant issue, that extra 5-10°F can push the compressor past its limit.

**Check**: Note whether the trips happen only between 2-5 PM on the hottest days. If the system runs fine in the morning and evening but shuts down mid-afternoon, ambient temperature is a contributing factor, not the root cause — something else is marginal.

### 5. Liquid Line Restriction

A partial blockage in the liquid line (drier clog, kinked line, TXV screen blockage) reduces refrigerant flow to the evaporator. The compressor pumps against a high pressure ratio, raising the discharge temperature.

### 6. Low Voltage (Brownout Condition)

South Florida's electric grid can drop voltage during peak summer afternoons. Low voltage increases current draw — the motor draws more amperage to produce the same power, which generates more heat. If voltage drops below 197V on a 240V system, the compressor may overheat even if everything else is normal.

**Check**: Have your utility company check voltage at the meter during peak hours. A 10% voltage drop doubles the current the compressor needs to start.

## Diagnostic Decision Tree

```
Compressor stops mid-cycle?
│
├─ Can you hear the contactor click?
│   ├─ YES → Compressor has power but won't start
│   │   ├─ Wait 20-30 min for cooling
│   │   ├─ Does it restart?
│   │   │   ├─ YES → Overload likely tripped → check causes above
│   │   │   └─ NO → Bad capacitor, open winding, or failed overload
│   │
│   └─ NO → Contactor not pulling in
│       ├─ Check thermostat, safety switches, control voltage
│       └─ Likely not an overload issue
│
├─ Does the outdoor fan run when the compressor stops?
│   ├─ YES → Fan working, compressor issue
│   └─ NO → Check condenser fan capacitor or motor
│
├─ Does it reset after ~30 minutes of cooling?
│   ├─ YES → Classic thermal overload cycling
│   └─ NO → Overload may have failed open (replace)
```

## What NOT to Do

- **Do not bypass the thermal overload** by jumping the safety circuit. This will destroy the compressor and can cause a fire.
- **Do not "help" the compressor start** by pushing the contactor manually. This can damage the starter components and is dangerous.
- **Do not add refrigerant** based on guesswork. Overcharging is as damaging as undercharging.
- **Do not install a "hard start kit"** without diagnosing why the overload is tripping. A hard start kit can mask the symptom and delay the real repair.

## When to Call a Licensed Technician

Thermal overload cycling is a **warning sign**, not a normal operating condition. If your AC is shutting off intermittently during hot afternoons:

- **Call for [emergency AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/)** if the unit won't run at all after waiting 30 minutes
- **Schedule a diagnostic visit** — an experienced technician can measure superheat, subcooling, amperage draw, and voltage to pinpoint the exact cause
- **Ask about coil cleaning** — a thorough condenser coil cleaning resolves the majority of summer overload trips in South Florida

Most thermal overload issues are solved with a condenser coil cleaning, capacitor replacement, or minor refrigerant adjustment — relatively low-cost repairs that prevent a $2,000+ compressor failure if ignored.
