# AC Refrigerant Types — What Homeowners Need to Know

Your AC system uses refrigerant to absorb heat from indoor air and release it outside. The type of refrigerant matters for maintenance costs, environmental impact, and system compatibility.

## Common Residential Refrigerants

### R-22 (Freon)
- **Status:** BANNED — Production and import ended January 1, 2020
- **Still in use:** Older systems (pre-2010) may still contain R-22
- **Cost:** $50-100+ per pound (reclaimed only) vs $5-10 historically
- **Impact:** Ozone-depleting substance (ODS)

**If your system uses R-22:** Every leak repair gets more expensive as supply shrinks. Consider replacement if your system needs a major refrigerant recharge.

### R-410A (Puron)
- **Status:** Current standard, being phased down
- **Used in:** Most systems installed 2010-2024
- **Cost:** $10-25 per pound
- **Impact:** No ozone depletion, but high global warming potential (GWP)

**If your system uses R-410A:** You're fine for the foreseeable future. New equipment will transition to lower-GWP alternatives, but R-410A will remain available for servicing existing systems.

### R-454B (Opteon XL41)
- **Status:** Next-generation replacement for R-410A
- **Used in:** New equipment starting 2025
- **Cost:** Similar to R-410A initially
- **Impact:** 78% lower GWP than R-410A

**What this means for you:** If you buy a new system after 2025, it will likely use R-454B. This is mildly flammable (A2L classification), requiring slightly different installation practices but no homeowner concern during normal operation.

## How to Find Your Refrigerant Type

1. **Check the data plate** on your outdoor condenser unit — it lists the refrigerant type
2. **Check your installation paperwork** or manual
3. **Ask your technician** during the next service visit

## Key Facts for Homeowners

- **You cannot mix refrigerant types.** An R-22 system cannot use R-410A and vice versa.
- **"Drop-in replacements" for R-22 exist** (R-407C, R-422D) but are not truly equivalent. Performance and reliability may differ. Discuss with your technician.
- **Refrigerant doesn't get "used up."** If your system needs a recharge, it has a leak. Adding refrigerant without fixing the leak is throwing money away.
- **Only EPA-certified technicians** can legally purchase and handle refrigerant. This is federal law (Section 608 of the Clean Air Act).
- **Venting refrigerant is illegal** with fines up to $44,539 per day per violation.

## Cost Comparison

| Refrigerant | Cost Per Pound | Typical Recharge (3-ton) | Total Cost |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------------|------------|
| R-22 | $50-100+ | 6-8 lbs | $300-800+ |
| R-410A | $10-25 | 8-12 lbs | $80-300 |
| R-454B | $15-30 (est) | 6-10 lbs | $90-300 |

*Costs include labor. Actual prices vary by market and contractor.*

## The Bottom Line

- **R-22 system?** Start planning for replacement. Each repair gets more expensive.
- **R-410A system?** You're in good shape. Maintain it well and expect 15-20 year life.
- **Buying new?** R-454B systems will become standard. Ask about availability and ensure your contractor is certified for A2L refrigerants.

---

*Never attempt to handle refrigerant yourself. Beyond being illegal, refrigerant contact causes severe frostbite, and inhalation can be fatal in enclosed spaces.*
