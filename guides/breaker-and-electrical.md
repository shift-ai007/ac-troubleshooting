# AC Won't Start: Breaker & Electrical Panel Guide

When your AC won't turn on, the electrical system is the first place to check — and it's one of the few things a homeowner can safely diagnose without professional help. This guide walks you through breaker checks, GFCI resets, and when to stop and call an electrician or HVAC tech.

**Safety note**: This guide covers the breaker panel and disconnect box only. Never open the electrical compartment on your AC unit itself — that contains capacitors that store lethal voltage even when the system is off.

## Step 1: Check the Thermostat First

Before touching the electrical panel, rule out the simplest causes:

- Is the thermostat set to COOL mode (not OFF or HEAT)?
- Is the set temperature at least 3 degrees below the room temperature?
- If the thermostat display is blank, replace the batteries
- Some thermostats have a 5-minute compressor delay — wait and listen

If the thermostat checks out but the system still won't start, move to the breaker panel.

## Step 2: Locate Your AC Breakers

Air conditioning systems typically have two breakers in your main electrical panel:

1. **Condenser breaker** (outdoor unit): Usually a 240V double-pole breaker, labeled "AC," "A/C," or "Condenser." Typically 30-60 amps.

2. **Air handler/furnace breaker** (indoor unit): May be a single-pole 120V breaker or a double-pole 240V. Often labeled "Furnace," "Air Handler," or "Fan."

Both breakers must be ON for the system to work. The indoor air handler fan is what pushes air through your ducts — even if the outdoor unit is fine, the system won't run without the air handler.

## Step 3: Reset the Breakers Correctly

**The correct way to reset a tripped breaker**:

1. Push the breaker handle firmly to the OFF position
2. Wait 5 seconds
3. Push it firmly to the ON position

A breaker that has tripped will often sit in a middle position between ON and OFF. You can't simply push it back to ON — you must go through OFF first.

**After resetting**: Go back to the thermostat, ensure it's set to COOL, and wait 5 minutes. The compressor has a built-in delay after a power interruption.

## Step 4: If the Breaker Trips Again Immediately

A breaker that trips as soon as (or within seconds of) the AC starting indicates a **short circuit** or **ground fault** in the equipment. This is a serious electrical fault.

**Do NOT reset the breaker more than once.** Repeatedly resetting a breaker into a short circuit can damage the equipment further, cause arcing, and create a fire risk. Call an HVAC technician or electrician.

If the breaker stays on for minutes or hours before tripping again, the cause is more likely an **overload** — a failing compressor, dirty condenser coil causing high amp draw, or a weak breaker that needs replacement. Still requires a professional, but the urgency is somewhat lower.

## Step 5: Check the Outdoor Disconnect Box

Next to your outdoor condenser unit, there should be a disconnect box — a small gray metal box mounted on the wall. It contains either a pull-out handle or a lever switch.

- **Pull-out type**: Grasp the handle and pull it straight out. Inspect the metal contacts for corrosion. Push it firmly back in.
- **Lever type**: Flip the lever to OFF, wait 10 seconds, then flip to ON.

Sometimes the disconnect is the problem — a partially-inserted pull-out or corroded contacts can prevent power from reaching the unit even though the main breaker is fine.

## Step 6: GFCI Outlets Near the Air Handler

Many newer homes have a GFCI outlet near the indoor air handler or in the attic. If this GFCI has tripped, the air handler won't run. Check any outlet within 6 feet of your indoor unit and press the RESET button.

A tripped GFCI usually means there's moisture reaching a connection. Have the condensate drain and any nearby plumbing checked.

## Step 7: Check for a Shutoff Switch

Some installations have a service disconnect switch mounted on or near the air handler — often a simple light-switch-style toggle. Make sure it's in the ON position. These switches are sometimes accidentally turned off during attic storage access or renovations.

## When the Breaker Isn't the Problem

If all breakers are ON, the disconnect is in place, and the system still won't start, the most common causes are:

| Symptom | Possible Cause | Action |
|---------|---------------|--------|
| Outdoor unit hums but fan doesn't spin | Failed capacitor | Call HVAC tech |
| Outdoor unit silent, indoor fan works | Contactor failure, thermostat wiring break | Call HVAC tech |
| Both units silent, thermostat is dark | Transformer failure (inside air handler) | Call HVAC tech |
| System runs then shuts off after 30 min | Dirty condenser coil, high-pressure cutoff | Clean debris, call for service |
| System blew cold yesterday, dead today | Mouse chewed low-voltage wire | Call HVAC tech |

## Electrical Panel Safety Tips

- Keep the area around your electrical panel clear (3 feet of clearance is code)
- Know where your main breaker is — it shuts off power to the entire house
- If you smell burning plastic near the panel, turn off the main breaker and call an electrician immediately
- Hold a flashlight in your mouth or use a headlamp — keep one hand free
- Never touch the panel or breakers with wet hands (Florida humidity in garages is a real issue)
- If your panel is a Federal Pacific, Zinsco, or Pushmatic brand (common in older Florida homes), these panels have known safety issues — have them evaluated by an electrician

## How to Label Your Panel for Next Time

While you're at the panel, take 5 minutes to label your breakers properly:

1. Take a photo of the current labels
2. Turn on every light and small appliance in the house
3. Flip breakers one at a time, note what turns off
4. Write clear labels — not "bedroom" but "front bedroom + hall light"
5. Use a label maker or permanent marker on a panel schedule card

This saves hours of confusion the next time something trips.
