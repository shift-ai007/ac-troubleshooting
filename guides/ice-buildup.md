# Ice on AC Unit — Frost or Ice on Pipes or Outdoor Unit

Ice on your AC is never normal. If you see frost or ice anywhere on the system, stop using it and diagnose immediately.

## Why Ice Forms

Your AC's evaporator coil gets cold — below 32°F — to remove heat from indoor air. Normally, the coil stays just above freezing and condensation drips off. Ice forms when the coil temperature drops too low, and the condensation freezes instead of draining.

There are exactly two reasons this happens:

### 1. Restricted Airflow (60% of cases)
The evaporator coil gets too cold because warm air isn't flowing across it fast enough. Common causes:
- **Clogged air filter** — The most common cause. Replace immediately.
- **Dirty evaporator coil** — Years of accumulated dust insulates the coil.
- **Closed or blocked vents** — Not enough warm air reaching the return.
- **Collapsed ductwork** — Air can't return to the system.

### 2. Low Refrigerant (35% of cases)
As refrigerant leaks out, the pressure drops and the remaining refrigerant gets abnormally cold in the evaporator. This ice is usually concentrated on the suction line (the larger copper pipe) and the evaporator coil.

### 3. Mechanical Failure (5% of cases)
- Blower motor not running at full speed
- TXV (expansion valve) stuck open
- Blocked metering device

## What to Do

### Immediate Steps
1. **Turn the AC OFF** at the thermostat — switch to OFF, not just a higher temperature
2. **Switch the fan to ON** — this will run the blower to help melt the ice faster
3. **Wait 4-6 hours** for the ice to fully melt — do NOT chip or scrape ice off (you'll damage the coil)

### Check After Thawing
1. Replace the air filter before restarting
2. Make sure all vents are open
3. Turn the AC back on and see if ice returns

### If Ice Returns
If ice forms again within 24 hours, the issue is:
- **Low refrigerant** — requires a leak search and repair by a licensed tech
- **Dirty coil** — requires a professional coil cleaning
- **Blower issue** — fan motor or capacitor problem

## Running AC with Ice Damages the Compressor

The compressor is designed to pump gas, not liquid. Liquid refrigerant returning from an iced coil can enter the compressor and destroy the valves. This is why you should turn the system off immediately.
