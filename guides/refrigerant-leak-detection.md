# AC Refrigerant Leak Detection: A Homeowner's Guide

Refrigerant leaks are one of the most common AC failures in South Florida — and one of the hardest to diagnose without professional equipment. A system that's low on refrigerant can't properly cool your home, runs longer and harder, and risks permanent compressor damage if left unchecked.

This guide helps you spot the early signs of a refrigerant leak before it destroys your compressor, understand why leaks happen, and decide when it's worth repairing versus replacing the system.

## How Refrigerant Affects Cooling

Your AC system doesn't "consume" refrigerant. Unlike oil in a car, refrigerant is a sealed-loop substance that cycles between liquid and gas states to absorb and release heat. A properly charged system maintains the exact amount of refrigerant it was charged with at installation.

When a leak occurs, the system loses refrigerant gradually — or catastrophically in the case of a large puncture. The compressor has to work harder to circulate what remains, and the evaporator coil can't absorb enough heat because there isn't enough liquid refrigerant expanding inside it.

The result: your AC blows lukewarm air, runs continuously, and the compressor overheats from the extra workload.

## Why South Florida Sees More Leaks

South Florida's environment is uniquely hard on refrigerant systems:

- **Salt air corrosion** (within 3-5 miles of either coast) eats through copper evaporator and condenser coils — the most common leak point.
- **Concrete slab mounting** — Outdoor units on concrete slabs in Florida develop micro-vibrations that wear through copper lines where they contact the slab edge.
- **Flooding and debris** — Storm surge and hurricane debris can puncture exposed line sets. Even standing water around the outdoor unit accelerates corrosion at the service valve connections.
- **Rapid temperature swings** — A system running at 40°F evaporator temperature at night and 95°F condenser temperature by afternoon undergoes thermal expansion cycles that stress brazed joints.
- **R-22 systems** — Pre-2010 systems using R-22 refrigerant have older rubber seals that dry out and crack faster in Florida's heat. Once R-22 is gone, replacement is prohibitively expensive (the remaining recycled supply costs $80-$150 per pound).

## How to Spot a Refrigerant Leak

### Symptom 1: Gradual Cooling Loss
The most reliable symptom. Your AC cools less effectively over days or weeks. At first you barely notice — the kitchen feels a bit warmer in the afternoon. By the time you actively feel uncomfortable, the system may have lost 20-30% of its charge.

**Test**: Set your thermostat to 78°F on a hot afternoon. Measure the air temperature at the supply register closest to the indoor unit. The supply air should be 15-20°F cooler than the return air at the filter grille. If the temperature difference (delta T) is under 14°F when outdoor temps are above 85°F, suspect low refrigerant.

### Symptom 2: Ice on the Evaporator Coil
Low refrigerant causes the evaporator coil to run colder than designed — so cold that humidity freezes on the coil surface rather than draining away. See our [ice buildup guide](guides/ice-buildup.md) for full diagnostic steps.

**Distinguishing ice from frost**: Frost on the copper suction line near the indoor unit is strongly suggestive of low refrigerant. Ice built up on the coil surface with normal airflow usually means an airflow problem, but if you've confirmed the filter is clean and registers are open, low refrigerant is the next likely cause.

### Symptom 3: Hissing or Bubbling Sounds
A substantial leak makes an audible hissing sound. If the leak is large enough, you may hear bubbling from inside the indoor unit — refrigerant boiling off as it escapes.

**When to check**: Listen near the outdoor unit's service valves and the indoor coil cabinet while the system is running. A quiet hiss is normal for a few seconds after startup (pressure equalization), but continuous hissing points to a leak.

### Symptom 4: Oil Stains or Grease Spots
Refrigerant oil (POE or mineral oil) travels through the system with the refrigerant. When refrigerant leaks, a small amount of oil escapes too. Look for:

- Oily residue on copper lines near the outdoor unit
- Grease spots near the evaporator coil access panel
- Pooled oil on the ground under the outdoor unit's service valve area
- A shiny, wet-looking patch on the condenser coil surface

**Important**: The oil is a tracer, not the problem itself. A spot of oil means refrigerant is escaping even if the system still seems to cool OK. Don't ignore it.

### Symptom 5: Compressor Short Cycling
Modern ACs have low-pressure safety switches. When refrigerant drops below a certain pressure, the switch cycles the compressor off to prevent damage. The compressor tries again, runs for 30-90 seconds, triggers the safety switch, and shuts down.

**The pattern**: System turns on → cools briefly → shuts off → waits → tries again → shuts off. If you hear your system starting and stopping every 2-3 minutes, and the outdoor air isn't unusually hot, low refrigerant is the prime suspect.

## Professional Leak Detection Methods

If you've confirmed the symptoms point to low refrigerant, a licensed technician will use one or more of these methods:

### Electronic Leak Detector
A handheld sniffer that detects refrigerant molecules. Most sensitive for large leaks (above 0.5 oz/year). Works well for accessible areas around the outdoor unit and service valves. Less effective for evaporator coil leaks buried inside the air handler cabinet.

### UV Dye Injection
A fluorescent dye is injected into the system and circulates with the refrigerant. After the system runs for 15-30 minutes, a UV light reveals the leak location. Most HVAC contractors use this method because it finds even pinhole leaks and works on both indoor and outdoor coils.

**Cost**: Typically included in the diagnostic fee ($89-$150). The dye stays in the system permanently and doesn't affect performance — but inject too much and it can clog the expansion valve orifice. Reputable techs use 1-2 drops maximum.

### Nitrogen Pressure Test
The system is pumped with dry nitrogen to 150-350 PSI and monitored for pressure drop over 15-60 minutes. If pressure holds, the system is sealed. If it drops, soap bubbles reveal the exact leak point.

**Best for**: Confirming a system IS or ISN'T leaking before committing to a repair. A nitrogen test costs about the same as a standard diagnostic call and should be step one before buying refrigerant.

### Vacuum Pull
After repair, the system is pulled into a deep vacuum (500 microns or lower) and held for 15-30 minutes. A rising vacuum reading indicates residual moisture or an unsealed leak. This isn't a primary detection method but tells the technician whether the repair actually worked.

## Repair vs Replace Decision

Refrigerant leak repair is one of the few AC repairs where replacement often wins over fixing. Here's the breakdown:

### Worth Repairing
- **Service valve leak** ($200-$400) — A new Schrader core or valve stem seal
- **Line set fitting at outdoor unit** ($300-$600) — Re-flare or replace the fitting
- **Brazed joint failure** ($400-$800) — Re-braze with Sil-Phos, vacuum, and recharge

### Consider Replacement
- **Evaporator coil leak** ($1,800-$2,800 for coil + labor + refrigerant) — On a system under 8 years old, worth it. Over 10 years, you're putting a new coil on an old compressor.
- **Condenser coil leak** ($2,200-$3,500) — The condenser holds more refrigerant. Once the main coil body is compromised, replacement is almost always the call.
- **Multiple leaks** — A system with leaks in two different locations has a systemic corrosion problem. Fix one, another pops up months later. [New AC installation](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-installation/) is the right move.

### The R-22 Factor
If your system uses R-22 refrigerant (manufactured before 2010):

- R-22 costs $80-$150 per pound (vs $10-$30/lb for R-410A)
- A typical charge is 6-10 pounds
- If your system needs 3+ pounds to recharge, the cost alone covers a significant portion of a new R-410A system
- New R-410A systems are 30-50% more efficient, so the monthly energy savings offset the replacement cost

**Bottom line**: If you have an R-22 system with an evaporator or coil leak, [AC replacement](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-replacement/) is almost always the financially correct decision.

## Can You Prevent Refrigerant Leaks?

You can't prevent all leaks — coil corrosion is a function of time and environment — but these steps significantly reduce your risk:

1. **Annual professional inspection** — A technician can spot early corrosion on coils and service valves before they become active leaks. Catching a pinhole before it becomes a gusher turns a $400 repair into a $200 one.
2. **Coil cleaning twice a year** — Corrosion accelerates when dirt and salt are trapped against the coil surface. A gentle rinse with a garden hose (not a pressure washer) removes corrosive deposits. In coastal areas, this is critical.
3. **Keep the outdoor unit dry** — Install a concrete pad with proper drainage. Don't let irrigation spray hit the unit. If your unit sits in a flood-prone area, consider raising it 6-12 inches on a stand.
4. **Protect accessible copper lines** — Wrap exposed line set insulation with UV-resistant tape. Sun-degraded insulation traps moisture against the copper, accelerating corrosion.
5. **Schedule professional maintenance** — A licensed technician performing a tune-up will check refrigerant pressures as part of the standard [AC maintenance service](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/). The cost of the visit ($99-$199) is cheaper than the refrigerant alone in a leak scenario.

## Urgency: When to Call Immediately

A slow refrigerant leak (losing 1-2 pounds per year) isn't an emergency — you have time to plan the repair or replacement. But call for [emergency AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/) if:

- The system is blowing warm air in >90°F weather
- You hear bubbling or gurgling sounds from the indoor unit
- The compressor is cycling on and off rapidly (short cycling)
- Ice is visible on the copper lines or outdoor unit
- You smell something like nail polish remover near the indoor unit (this can indicate refrigerant decomposition from overheating)

Running a seriously low AC for hours or days can destroy the compressor. If you suspect a significant leak, turn the system off at the thermostat and the breaker, and call a professional.
